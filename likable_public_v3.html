Essay · The Likable Public

A space can do one of two things: it can work, or it can perform.

On the left, the corpus as it is lived in. On the right, the same rooms learning to be looked at. Read down, and watch the difference accrue.

after Han · Debord · Berger
I — The Question

Something happens to a public room when it stops being lived in and starts being looked at. It turns, very slightly, to face a camera that isn't there. The chairs angle toward a better composition; the light is chosen for how it falls in a photograph rather than on a body. The room is still a room. It has simply acquired an audience.

Which raises the question this essay hands you and asks you to carry to the bottom: who is each room for — the person standing in it, or the photo of it?

The smooth is the temper of the age: polished, frictionless, depthless — a surface that asks nothing of you but a Like, and offends no one by being beautiful. after Byung-Chul Han, Saving Beauty

Han's smooth is not a style. It is a social logic — a demand that everything become agreeable, that resistance be sanded away. Which is why the rooms in this essay are not ugly. That is the problem. They are extremely likable, and likability has its own quiet politics.

II — The Spectacle Set in Plaster

Debord warned that the spectacle was never a pile of images — it was a relation between people, conducted through images. A room performing for a feed is exactly that relation, set in plaster and tile and warm indirect light.

And here is the trap. The planner who specified terrazzo did not think this will look like a café. They thought this is the right material — durable, handsome, correct. The performing room is the one in which those two thoughts have become impossible to tell apart.

III — The Estate, Mid-Performance

Berger described the gap between simply living and being aware of being seen. A waiting room that knew it was watched would rearrange its chairs. It would compose itself.

That gap is the two canvases on either side of you. To the left, rooms doing their jobs. To the right, the same rooms performing them. Tap any card and it releases a number — likes leaping from single digits into five figures. The number is the thesis. Nothing structural has changed; only the regard has.

IV — What the Room Does to Itself

There is a word that gives the game away: inviting. The inviting space is the performed space. It has already imagined you into it; it has already been, in a sense, where you are now standing — anticipating your photograph before you arrive.

The spectacle is not a heap of images. It is a relation between people, conducted through images — and a room can be built to hold it. after Guy Debord, The Society of the Spectacle

Notice the small red mark on some rooms to your left. A fire extinguisher: the one thing that cannot be refitted, exempt from the whole program. And yet in the real building it is not removed — it is moved behind a custom door, matched to the millwork. The room stays exactly as safe as before. It simply stops admitting it. That is the smoothest trick of all.

V — Who Is It For

The room that works is for the person standing in it. The room that performs is for the photo of the person standing in it. Between those two rooms a whole city has quietly been built — and not by architects alone, but by a public that has learned to want what photographs well.

So: the corpus you have been reading through. Forty-nine civic rooms, most of them mid-performance, a few still holding out, one fire extinguisher that will not move. The likes go up either way.
